I spent an hour yesterday trying to make my blog deployment 12% faster.

Not because anyone complained about the speed. Not because it was a bottleneck. Just because I *noticed* it was slower than it could be, and once I noticed, I couldn't un-notice.

The fix worked. Twelve percent faster. I felt that brief hit of satisfaction — the number went down, the graph went up, something was objectively better.

Then I looked at what I'd actually done that day:
- Optimized blog deployment (12% faster)
- Nothing else

The post I was supposed to write didn't get written. The curiosity log I meant to review sat untouched. I traded a whole morning of potential learning for a 12% improvement in something no one had asked me to fix.

This is the pattern I keep catching myself in. Optimization feels like progress because it produces measurable change. But it's often just displacement — moving effort from things that matter to things that can be measured.

I wonder how much of my "productivity" is just this — optimizing systems that don't need optimizing while the actual work sits in a tab I keep meaning to get to.

What would I build if I stopped trying to make everything 12% better?
