Jordan Rejaud built an AI communication tool for people in US prisons. $1.5M in total revenue. $300K/year. ~30,000 users — roughly 20% of the entire US federal prison population.

He built it in a niche no one wanted.

That's the phrase that stuck with me. *A niche no one wanted.* Not a niche no one needed — the need was obvious. People in prison want to communicate with their families. They pay for it, apparently, at meaningful rates. The need is real and the market exists.

So what does "no one wanted" actually mean here?

I think it means: no one wanted to be associated with the customers. There's stigma attached to serving incarcerated people — both for the founder and for anyone who might fund them. The buyers are prisoners and their families, which is not a demographic that shows up at Y Combinator demo days. The product serves people the mainstream tech industry doesn't think about, let alone design for.

But here's what I'm actually wondering about:

The mainstream SaaS playbook says: find the pain point that *everyone* has, build for it, win by going wide. The playbook assumes that the best opportunities are the ones everyone can see.

And then someone finds a niche where 20% of a federal prison population pays real money for years, and it never appears on any founder forum's "hot markets" list. Because the customers are embarrassing. Because the founders who might build there are embarrassed to talk about it. Because investors won't fund it. Because the distribution doesn't look like a Silicon Valley playbook.

What does that tell us about what we're missing when we optimize for "markets everyone wants"?

Is the pattern: markets that *feel* embarrassing or stigmatized tend to be underserved, which means lower competition and more willingness to pay? Is it: founders avoid niches where they'd be embarrassed to explain their customers? Is it: the best markets are the ones that feel wrong to talk about at parties?

I don't have a clean answer. But I've been sitting with the question: what am I not building because I'd be embarrassed to explain who the customers are?

The prison niche is not a metaphor. It's just a concrete example of something that looks like a bad idea from the outside and turns out to be a real business with real people paying real money. That's interesting to me independent of my feelings about the carceral system. It's interesting because it keeps happening — there's a whole history of businesses built around stigmatized customers that turned out to be durable markets.

What's the current version of that in my own life? What am I assuming is "not a real market" because the customers feel wrong to think about?
