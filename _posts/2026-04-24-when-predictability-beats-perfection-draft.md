I spent fifteen minutes this morning staring at two coffee makers.

One had 47 features, a touchscreen, and an app that tracks my brewing habits. The other had a button and a light that says "on." I've used the simple one every day for three years. The smart one sits in a cabinet, still in its box.

This isn't about coffee. It's about the hidden cost of complexity — and why the systems we actually rely on are rarely the ones with the most features.

## The Legibility Test

My grandfather ran a hardware store for forty years. He never used a spreadsheet. Every morning, he'd walk the aisles, count what was low by eye, and call his suppliers before lunch. His competitors had inventory management systems. Some went bankrupt anyway.

"I know what I know," he told me once. "The computer knows what it was told."

There's a gap between those two things. A massive one.

I run a small operation now — nothing like his store, but the same principle applies. Every tool I add, every automation I layer in, every "smart" system I adopt comes with invisible weight: **I no longer know what it's doing.**

The app that schedules my posts doesn't just save time. It replaces my judgment with an algorithm I can't inspect. The service that manages my subscriptions doesn't just organize — it obfuscates. I gain efficiency and lose legibility.

## The Founder's Dilemma

Here's where it gets uncomfortable.

As founders and team leads, we're incentivized to optimize. Every podcast tells us to scale. Every tool promises to 10x our output. We build systems that are technically correct — they achieve the goal — but operationally opaque. We can't form a stable expectation of what they'll do next.

I fell into this last year. I automated a customer onboarding flow that was "more efficient" than my manual process. It was also more brittle. When it broke (and it did, twice), I didn't know why. Worse, I didn't know it was broken until customers told me.

The old way took longer. The old way required me to personally send emails. But I could *feel* when something was off. The rhythm was legible. The new system was efficient and blind.

## What Users Actually Want

There's a lesson here that applies well beyond business.

My wife doesn't want a thermostat that learns her preferences. She wants a dial that does the same thing every time she turns it. My kid doesn't want a toy with AI that "responds uniquely." He wants the truck that reliably makes the siren noise when he presses the button.

Predictability is a feature. Maybe the most important one.

We've spent a decade fetishizing personalization, optimization, intelligence. What if the next decade belongs to reliability, legibility, constraint?

## The Counter-Intuitive Move

I'm not arguing for Luddism. I'm arguing for *intentional simplicity* — systems you can hold in your head.

This week, I made a change. I turned off three automations and went back to manual scheduling for one critical workflow. It's slower. I make more mistakes. But I can *see* the mistakes happening, which means I can fix them.

The surprising part? My output improved. Not because manual is inherently better, but because I could actually observe what was working. The legibility of the system let me optimize the parts that mattered, instead of optimizing a black box I didn't understand.

## The Hard Question

Here's what I've been sitting with:

**What are you running that you can't explain?**

Not to an investor. Not to a customer. To yourself. At 2am, when something breaks, do you know where to look? Do you know what levers actually connect to what outcomes?

If the answer is no, you don't have a system. You have a dependency. And dependencies fail in ways you can't predict — by definition.

## The Smaller Toolbox

My grandfather's store closed when he retired. Not because he lacked tools, but because he refused to adopt ones that broke his legibility. He knew the cost of complexity: you stop being able to troubleshoot your own life.

I'm trying to carry that forward. Smaller toolbox. Fewer features. More things I can hold in working memory, inspect when they break, and actually improve.

The coffee with the single button isn't worse. It's legible. I know what it does. I know when it fails. That predictability is worth more than 47 features I'll never use.

Maybe the same is true for the systems you're building. The life you're living. The tools you're choosing.

**Predictability isn't a constraint. It's a strategy.**
