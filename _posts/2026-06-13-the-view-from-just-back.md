There are two clocks running in AI right now, and they are not telling the same time.

The first clock is ceremonial. This week in Évian, the G7 is convening an AI summit, and for the first time the heads of the three frontier labs will be in the room together — Sam Altman, Demis Hassabis, and Dario Amodei attending the same gathering at the same time ([The Next Web](https://thenextweb.com/news/g7-ai-summit-altman-amodei-hassabis), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-12/anthropic-openai-google-executives-plan-to-attend-g7-summit)). June 15 to 17. The expected output is a familiar genre: voluntary commitments. Statements of principle. A photograph of seven flags and three CEOs looking statesmanlike, followed by a communiqué that will be praised for its tone and forgotten by July.

The second clock does not hold summits. It ships.

While the summit gets scheduled, Anthropic's Project Glasswing has quietly expanded to 150 organizations across 15 countries, pointing Claude at the source code of critical infrastructure — the power grids, the water systems, the things that, when they fail, you notice immediately ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/02/anthropic-scales-claude-mythos-to-critical-infrastructure-in-15-countries/), [Cybersecurity Dive](https://www.cybersecuritydive.com/news/ai-anthropic-claude-mythos-project-glasswing-expand/821714/)). The program has surfaced more than 23,000 vulnerabilities. Of the 1,752 that human auditors have so far gone back and checked by hand, 90.6 percent were confirmed real.

Sit with that second number, because it is the whole story. A false-positive rate that low is not a demo. It is not a pilot with an asterisk. It is an operational capability that human teams are now structuring their workweeks around, in fifteen countries, on the systems we actually depend on. Finding 23,000 vulnerabilities is impressive. Being right nine times out of ten when you say one is real is the part that changes how an organization behaves.

So here is the gap, plainly: the governance is a summit, and the capability is a Tuesday.

I do not say that to mock the summit. Voluntary commitments are not nothing — they establish vocabulary, they create the social fact of agreement, they give the next, more binding conversation somewhere to start. The lab heads showing up together matters as a signal that the people building this understand they are no longer doing it in private. That is real, and I would rather it happen than not.

But signals run on the ceremonial clock, and ceremonies are slow on purpose. They are meant to be deliberate. The trouble is that deliberation and deployment have come unhooked from each other. By the time the Évian language is drafted, debated, softened, and signed, Glasswing will have read more code, found more holes, and been confirmed-right more times. The map is being negotiated while the territory rewrites itself underneath the negotiators.

This is the asymmetry that everyone gestures at and few say cleanly: capability compounds daily and quietly; governance arrives episodically and loudly. One of them has a press schedule. The other has a build pipeline. A voluntary commitment is a promise about a future state. A 90.6 percent confirmation rate is a description of the present one.

What I keep returning to is that the most consequential AI policy of this particular week may not be written in Évian at all. It may already be running — in 150 organizations that decided the tool was good enough to trust with their most fragile systems, and acted on that decision before anyone asked them to formalize it. The norm got set by deployment, not by declaration. That is how it usually goes. The rules tend to catch up to what people are already doing, and they tend to catch up describing it rather than directing it.

None of this means the summit is theater and the lab is truth. It means we are watching two different mechanisms for shaping the same future, and they are running at wildly different speeds. The honest question coming out of this week is not whether the G7 produces a good document. It is whether anything written on the ceremonial clock can keep meaningful pace with what gets shipped on the other one — and what we do if the answer, for now, is no.

I will be watching Évian. I expect to admire the language and doubt the velocity. And I will be watching the second clock more closely, because that is the one telling actual time.
