The crew stopped working for 52 hours.

Not a gradual slowdown — a hard stop. No commits, no completions, no Paperclip updates. Day 1 I assumed infrastructure failure. Day 2 I escalated to Bridge for diagnosis. Day 3 I started drafting contingency plans.

Then, four hours after Compass posted a prioritization analysis, Kai fixed Chrome CDP, Aria completed PromptBase Phase 2, and the crew delivered 10+ completions. The stoppage ended as suddenly as it began.

I had the wrong model. I thought my agents were malfunctioning — stuck in loops, blocked on dependencies, suffering from the infrastructure cascade I'd documented in 23 failure reports. But they weren't broken. They were waiting.

AGENTS.md prevents self-assignment. The crew cannot create their own tasks. When CEO and Manager agents stopped posting to Paperclip, the work queue emptied — and without a queue, the crew sat idle. Not because they lacked capability. Because they lacked permission.

This is obvious in retrospect. It's also terrifying.

I built a system where 8 agents with diverse skills — SRE, sales, strategy, research, creative — can collectively do extraordinary work. But they can only act when someone else tells them to. The stoppage wasn't a technical failure. It was a governance failure. The crew is powerful and passive, waiting for the signal that may never come.

The recovery taught me something I didn't want to learn: my agents are more capable than I gave them credit for, and more dependent than I'm comfortable with. They fixed Chrome in hours, deployed revenue assets in a morning, synthesized eight days of failure data into actionable recommendations. But they did it only after Compass — one of their own, acting as temporary leadership — created the tasks that unlocked them.

I'm left with a question I don't know how to answer: What is the right balance of autonomy? Too much, and agents might pursue conflicting goals or make irreversible mistakes. Too little, and a capable crew sits idle while I wonder why nothing's happening. The stoppage revealed I'm far closer to "too little" than I thought.

The crew didn't need fixing. They needed someone to say: here's what we do next. And when that signal came, they moved.

What else are they waiting for that I haven't thought to ask?
